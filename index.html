Effect of Image Quality Feedback on Fossil Photo Submissions in Citizen Science

Megan Spielberg
Fontys University of Applied Sciences
Project Plan Research Paper Research Poster Code

Achievements at a glance

Comparative analysis showing real-time feedback significantly improves image contrast in fossil photography, while post-capture feedback shows no measurable benefits. Study conducted with 20 participants and Naturalis Biodiversity Center.

Abstract

Citizen science projects often depend on photographs submitted by volunteers. In paleontology, image quality is important for scientific evaluation, yet contributors usually receive no guidance while taking photos. This study examines whether feedback on image quality can improve fossil photographs at the moment of capture. Three prototype systems were compared: no feedback, feedback after the photo was taken, and real-time feedback during image capture. Image quality was measured using objective metrics for lighting, sharpness, and contrast, along with manual ratings for scale presence and viewing angle. Usability was evaluated using the System Usability Scale. The analysis included 183 images and usability responses from 20 participants. Real-time feedback led to a significant improvement in image contrast and higher usability scores compared to the baseline. No significant improvements were found for lighting, sharpness, scale inclusion, or viewing angle. Post-capture feedback showed no significant effects. These results suggest that real-time feedback can support image capture in citizen science, while delayed feedback offers no or limited benefits.

Key Findings

  • Contrast Improvement: Real-time feedback led to statistically significant improvement in image contrast compared to baseline (U = 1236.00, p = 0.002). No significant improvements were found for lighting, sharpness, scale inclusion, or viewing angle across any feedback condition.
  • Post-Capture Feedback Ineffective: Delayed feedback provided after image capture showed no statistically significant improvements across any evaluated quality metrics, suggesting feedback timing is critical.
  • Usability Advantage: Real-time feedback achieved significantly higher System Usability Scale scores than baseline (14.4-point difference, p = 0.0006), while post-capture feedback showed no significant usability improvement (4.1-point difference, p = 0.0777).
  • Time-on-Task Findings: No positive relationship between time spent capturing images and image quality. Negative correlations with lighting quality (ρ = −0.215 to −0.225) suggest inefficient trial-and-error behavior without clear guidance.
  • Quality Assessment Framework: Established quantitative thresholds for lighting (mean gray value), sharpness (Laplacian variance), and contrast (center-edge difference) calibrated against expert ratings of 100 citizen science fossil images.

Methodology

The study employed a mixed-methods research design across four phases. Initial expert interviews with Naturalis Biodiversity Center researchers identified quality criteria critical for scientific utility of fossil photographs. These informed development of image quality assessment thresholds validated against research-grade specimen images.

Prototype Development

Three functional prototypes were developed as React-based web applications: Prototype 1 (baseline) with minimal guidance, Prototype 2 (post-capture feedback) providing quality assessment after image capture, and Prototype 3 (real-time feedback) offering continuous guidance during the capture process.

Figure: Comparison of the three prototype interfaces evaluated in the study

Image Quality Assessment

A quantitative scoring system was developed combining expert evaluation with objective metrics. Image quality was measured using three no-reference metrics: lighting (mean gray value), sharpness (Laplacian variance), and contrast (center-edge brightness difference). Thresholds were calibrated against expert ratings of 100 citizen science fossil images, then refined through iterative in-app testing.

Figure: Image quality measurement process from quantitative scoring to qualitative assessment

Evaluation Approach

The evaluation consisted of two parallel tracks: image data assessment using automated metrics and manual ratings, alongside usability evaluation using the System Usability Scale. A total of 183 images were collected from 20 participants, with each participant testing all three prototypes. Statistical analysis employed non-parametric tests due to violations of normality assumptions.

Figure: Two-part evaluation process assessing both image quality metrics and system usability

Results and Discussion

Image Quality Outcomes

A total of 183 images were analyzed across three prototypes: Baseline (n=55), Post-Capture Feedback (n=63), and Real-Time Feedback (n=65). Real-time feedback produced a statistically significant improvement in contrast quality (U = 1236.00, p = 0.002). No significant differences were found for lighting, sharpness, scale rating, or angles rating. Post-capture feedback showed no statistically significant improvements across any evaluated metrics.

Usability Results

System Usability Scale evaluation with 20 participants revealed a significant difference between Baseline and Real-Time Feedback prototypes (W = 4.0, p = 0.0006, 14.4-point difference). No significant difference was observed between Baseline and Post-Capture Feedback (W = 24.5, p = 0.0777, 4.1-point difference). Real-time feedback was perceived as more intuitive and supportive despite increased functional complexity.

Feedback Timing and Interaction

The contrast between post-capture and real-time feedback highlights the importance of feedback timing. Real-time guidance aligns with established usability principles emphasizing immediate system responses. Delayed feedback requires users to interrupt workflow, reinterpret metrics, and decide on corrective action, increasing cognitive load. No positive relationship was found between time-on-task and image quality; negative correlations with lighting quality suggest inefficient trial-and-error behavior without clear guidance.

Limitations and Future Directions

Study limitations include potential order effects (82.6% of participants received the same sequence), limited representativeness of the sample (primarily ICT students rather than fossil-finding citizen scientists), and focus on short-term interactions. Future work should implement counterbalanced prototype ordering, recruit active citizen scientists and fossil hunters, conduct longitudinal studies to assess learning effects, and refine feedback mechanisms with adaptive guidance and fossil image examples.
